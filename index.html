PART 00

The Operating System

Before a single tactic matters, the wiring underneath has to be right. These are the beliefs Yuri and Gene return to in almost every session — the difference between a rep who executes and one who improvises their way to a zero.

MindsetFoundation

Execution Beats Talent — Sell the Same Way on Your Worst Day

The rep who freestyles is their own biggest competitor.

The whole point of a script is to remove emotion from selling. Mood, energy, stress, being sick — none of it should move your numbers. Top performers sell the same way every single time. If your execution changes with your mood, your results will always be unstable, and you'll blame the leads instead of the process.

"A script is not to sound robotic. A script is to remove emotions from selling. If a new hire cannot read your script and perform at 70–80% effectiveness, the script is not good enough."

Yuri Saiko

Creativity comes after mastery, not before. Actors don't sound scripted because they've run the lines a hundred times — same rule here. And when a deal dies, the honest question is never "were the leads bad?" It's "did I execute correctly on the leads that were good?"

Key Takeaways
  • Repeatable > creative. Lock the motion, then add flair.
  • Complaining about leads is usually proof the script wasn't followed.
  • You won't close every lead (1 in 10–15 is normal). Judge yourself on execution against the good ones.
MindsetNewbie

Sell Certainty, Not Pain — Conviction Transfers

The newbie's real objection is never the price. It's "what if I fail again?"

People don't buy logic. They buy certainty. With inexperienced buyers especially, stop digging endlessly into pain — their true blocker is that they don't trust themselves. If you don't make them certain this is the best program, they will never find the money. And certainty is contagious: if you're unsure, they're unsure. If you believe, they follow.

"You're creating double pain: their current problem plus asking them to pay. If you don't create massive certainty this is the best solution, you're just torturing them for nothing."

Yuri Saiko

This is why conviction outranks clever lines. You can't overcome an objection you don't believe is beatable — your hesitation leaks through the phone. Yuri's own certainty comes from knowing the refund policy, the coaching, and the thousands of closed deals cold. Internalize those until belief is automatic.

Key Takeaways
  • Newbie's hidden objection = fear of failing again. Sell them on themselves.
  • Make strong statements — you can't build certainty by only asking questions.
  • "Would you agree this is the best program out there? If not, give me one reason why not."
MindsetSelf-Analysis

Competitiveness Is the #1 Predictor — Outrun the Slowest Gazelle

You're 5th on an 8-person team and not studying 3rd and 4th. That's the problem.

If a salesperson isn't competitive, the odds of being good are close to zero. The fix for the bottom of the board isn't mystery — it's comparison. Pull the transcripts of the reps just ahead of you, put them side by side with yours, and find the exact differences. Don't benchmark against the untouchable #1 who "talks too much" — benchmark against the replicable #2 and #3.

"The gazelle doesn't need to outrun the lion. She needs to outrun the slowest gazelle. That's the good news about being on the bottom — you can compare yourself."

Yuri Saiko

Generic self-assessment is worthless. "I need to close better" gets you nowhere. The work is specific: download ten of your demos, run them against a top rep's, and name the moves you're missing. Own your own data — buy your own AI, export your numbers, and study yourself like game film.

Key Takeaways
  • Compare transcripts side-by-side. Name specific gaps, not vibes.
  • Benchmark against the reachable rep above you, not the outlier at the top.
  • Deliberate practice > more reps. Isolate one skill, drill it, get feedback, apply live.
MindsetGene's Law

If You Didn't Get to a "No," You Didn't Sell

You presented the platform, asked for the smallest number, and they said yes. That's not selling.

Presentation is not sales. Sales starts after the first "no." Gene's sharpest diagnosis of the team: most reps demo the product and then ask for the lowest possible commitment. That's order-taking with extra steps. Getting to a "man, that's too much" is not failure — it's the minimum bar for having actually sold. A business owner who never feels the tension of a real number never makes a real decision.

"If you didn't get to 'man, that's too much' — if you didn't get to that point — you didn't even sell. Your title can basically be Senior Demo Presenter."

Gene Blinkov

"That's when his juices start flowing. Otherwise he's just sitting there falling asleep. You HAVE to get to a no. You have to get to the point of tension."

Gene Blinkov

You need to hear a NO. If you didn't get to a NO, you didn't sell — you presented.
Key Takeaways
  • The "no" / point of tension is the start of the sale, not the end of the call.
  • If a prospect sails through with zero pushback and doesn't buy, you never gave them anything real to react to.
  • "No objection" is the worst objection — it means you never asked for the money.
MindsetGene's Law

Don't Apologize for Their Money — They're Business Owners

You hear "I don't have a number in mind" and immediately offer $500. Worst possible move.

If you feel bad asking for $5,000, you will never ask for $5,000. Gene's challenge to the team is really about your internal story. "Broke" means different things to different people — the agent on your demo might have a rich spouse; the guy who says "$1,000" might be sitting on $50,000. You don't know, so stop deciding for them and stop protecting them from their own business decisions.

"You sound like you're apologizing in front of them. $500 is nothing. We spend $500 a minute over here. Stop sounding like you're apologizing for asking for the lowest $500."

Gene Blinkov

Business owners go broke — it's part of it. Your job is to help them find the path to the decision, not to save them from it. Fix the internal story first; the ask gets easy after that.

"I ask myself every single morning: where was I acting like a loser yesterday? And I go fix it. That's what working on yourself actually is."

Gene Blinkov

Key Takeaways
  • Never put a number in their mouth. Make them think about their own business.
  • "Broke" is relative and often untrue on intake forms — don't pre-disqualify.
  • Your discomfort with the ask is the real bottleneck. Rebuild the story.
Mindset2026 Reality

The Window for Easy Sales Is Closing — Learn to Actually Close

The AI SDR now calls every new user 2× a day for 10 days before a rep is even involved.

The era of scooping easy closes off a fresh list is ending. AI already out-books humans on the SDR task. That means the reps who survive are the ones who can genuinely close on a demo — not the ones who show up when the competition is thin and swoop the layups. Yuri said it plainly: get good quicker, or get replaced.

"You guys better start being good quicker or we'll be replacing people."

Yuri Saiko

Your defensible edge is the one thing AI can't do: build a real relationship with someone who didn't know ISTL existed until you called. That's the value. That's the commission argument. That's the job security.

"Hey, if I wouldn't have called this guy, you guys would never have gotten that money — that's the story you want to be telling."

Yuri Saiko

Key Takeaways
  • Booking is being automated. Closing is the skill that keeps you employed.
  • Order-takers (PPC/hot-inbound only) are the first to be automated away.
  • Human edge = relationships you create, not deals that fell into your lap.
PART 01

Before the Call

Most demos are won or lost before the prospect ever picks up. Speed to the phone, knowing the market, deciding what you're selling, and one genuine human detail — this is the prep that separates a controlled call from a fishing trip.

Pre-CallSpeed to Lead

The 30-Second Window Is the Only Guaranteed Pickup

You save new-user calls for the end of the day. By then they're cold.

The single highest-leverage activity in your day is calling a new user within 30 seconds of registration. It's the only moment on the entire platform where pickup is near-guaranteed, and it directly lifts downstream demo show rates. Most reps miss it because they're "organizing their pipeline" — which Yuri calls avoidance, not work.

"Unless you are on a demo right now, you should be calling new users. Not in two hours. Not at end of day. Now. Organizing a CRM full of people you're never going to call is avoidance, not work."

Yuri Saiko

Key Takeaways
  • Call within 30 seconds of registration — it's the only guaranteed pickup.
  • Self-booked demos show up at far higher rates than platform/AI-routed ones.
  • "Organizing" leads you'll never call is procrastination wearing a productivity costume.
Pre-CallResearch

Know the Market Before You Dial — Area Code Tells You the Play

You're explaining the marketplace, filters, and sale leads to a prospect with two leads in their county.

The first three digits of the phone number are the area code — which is the market. Check it before the call. If it's a thin market with a handful of leads, you already know the play: go straight to Auto Match, skip the full platform tour. If it's competitive, you know to anchor on volume and buy-box control. Deciding this before you dial is the difference between a guided demo and a fishing trip.

"If you have somebody with two leads in the market, you should know this before the call. Use all your willpower to push them to expand or go automatch. Don't explain sale leads to a two-lead market."

Yuri Saiko

Know the pricing reality of the market too. In hot metros there's no such thing as a cheap exclusive lead — say so early rather than letting the client set an impossible expectation.

Hot metros
CA / San Diego / Miami / Boston / Fresno: ~$200 floor, $275–375+ typical. $100 = zero leads.
Mid markets
Indiana & comparable: $100 possible, $150–225 for solid flow.
Universal heuristic
Ask what they pay on PropertyLeads. Start there.
Thin market
Go straight to Auto Match. Skip the walkthrough. Push expansion.
Key Takeaways
  • Area code → market → the play. Do this in the 3 seconds before you call.
  • Thin market = Auto Match only. Don't hand them a low-volume objection you didn't need to create.
  • Don't round your numbers ("17–29 leads" sounds real; "about 20" sounds scripted).
Pre-CallGene's Law

Three Minutes of Research Kills the "Corporate" Feel

Your calls feel generic because you walked in knowing nothing about the human on the other end.

Spend 3–5 minutes on LinkedIn, Instagram, or their background before every demo. Find one real, genuine detail — motorcycles, a niche, a side business — and open with it. Not fake flattery. Real curiosity. It's the fastest way to stop sounding like "corporate" and start sounding like a person the prospect wants to keep talking to.

"Research the person, connect like a human, trial close early, close directly, and work the no."

Gene Blinkov

Put their name and company on the title slide. Hold pricing and packages until they're sold. And you don't need to be a real estate expert — you need enough fluency to follow the conversation. Confidence kills uncertainty faster than knowledge does.

Before Every Demo — Checklist
  • Check LinkedIn / IG / background — find one genuine talking point.
  • Know their niche and market. Put their name on the title slide.
  • Ask yourself: "What am I applying from Gene / Yuri today?"
Pre-CallStrategy

Decide What You're Selling Before You Open Your Mouth

You tour the whole platform hoping something sticks, then wonder why they're confused.

Walk in knowing the one or two packages you intend to pitch. The budget question gives you the hint — use it, don't fixate on it. With a narrow market and a mid budget, the answer is usually Auto Match + a preload. Once you've decided, don't wander. Showing everything ("let me also show you the marketplace, Coupon Club, sale leads…") is how you talk yourself out of a deal and create objections you never needed.

"Know what you're trying to sell. If it's $499 — sell it as the best thing she's going to ever get. Don't show her the small one and say 'that's where you belong.'"

Yuri Saiko

Key Takeaways
  • Pin the 1–2 packages you'll pitch as early in discovery as possible.
  • Sell one thing as the best decision they'll ever make — don't tour the catalog.
  • Under $499 budget → Coupon Club only. Don't touch fixed price, balance mechanics, or sale leads.
FrameworkPre-Call

Know Your CTA Before You Call — Plan A, B, and C

Without a target outcome, you default to rambling and the prospect exits committing to nothing.

Every call needs three landing spots decided in advance. If you don't know which one you're driving toward, every sentence drifts. Each thing you say on the call should be pulling toward one of these outcomes:

Plan A
They register and look at the platform now.
Plan B
They schedule a one-on-one call / demo.
Plan C
They agree to stay in touch and accept a follow-up.

The word "might" is your friend for pulling toward any of them: "You might be eligible for a couple of free leads depending on your market." It's not a promise — it's curiosity bait that keeps them on the phone.

Key Takeaways
  • Write down A/B/C before you dial. Never wing the destination.
  • Every sentence should pull toward one of the three.
  • "Might" opens the door without over-promising.
Pre-CallProof

Pick the One Testimonial — and Sharpen What Only You Can Say

You send the same "guy closed $270K" screenshot everyone else sends. It blurs into noise.

For each demo, pick the single best testimonial where the prospect thinks "that's me." Know it by heart. Lead with the profile match — product, market, background — not the raw dollar figure. A Dallas flipper hearing about another Dallas flipper is far more persuasive than any guru endorsement (prospects assume gurus are paid). Build a market map: for every metro you sell into, know one name, one number, one result.

"You should know which success stories are in which market. John Looney = Dallas. Don't rely on RJ Bates / guru endorsements — prospects assume they're paid."

Yuri Saiko

Same discipline applies to your credibility. The whole team sends identical outreach, so nobody stands out. Write 3–5 specific things only you can say — not "I'm a top closer," but real, personal, memorable details. Good sales orgs will ask in interviews what made you different. If you don't have an answer, you don't get hired.

Key Takeaways
  • One profile-matched testimonial per demo, known cold. Lead with the match, not the money.
  • Frame it as a "recording / interview / training," never a "testimonial" (assumed paid).
  • Write your own 3–5 differentiators. Generic reps are interchangeable — and replaceable.
PART 02

The Opening

The first two minutes decide whether they trust you enough to tell you the truth. Credibility goes first, structure goes up front, and the sale is quietly won or lost long before anyone says the word "price."

OpeningScript

Open So They Can't Say "Not Interested"

"Hi, is this a good time?" hands them the exit on a silver platter.

Your opening should assume the reason they're there and give them an easy, low-threat yes. The word "considering" does the heavy lifting — it acknowledges they raised a hand without demanding commitment, so the reflexive "not interested" has nothing to grab onto.

Say it like this I see you were considering getting some motivated seller leads — I wanted to reach out personally before your account went cold. Do you have your phone or laptop handy for two minutes?

Notice the structure: reason for the call, a personal touch ("personally," "before it went cold"), and a small physical ask (phone/laptop) that gets them doing something instead of deciding whether to talk to you.

Key Takeaways
  • "Considering" lowers the threat and pre-frames their intent.
  • Open with a reason + a personal note + a tiny physical action.
  • Never ask a yes/no that lets them hang up ("is now a bad time?").
OpeningProof

Lead With the Number — 12,716 Contracts Signed

They don't know you, your accent, or your company. Give them a reason to keep listening.

Drop the platform's hard proof number early, as a hook — not as a closer. It's on the platform, in front of clients, so every rep should have it memorized and current. Used in the opening or the confirmation call, it reframes the whole conversation from "who is this person" to "this thing clearly works."

On the confirmation / opening call Did you know our users signed 12,716 contracts last year through the platform? Most people haven't seen that number — and it's exactly what I'm going to walk you through.

"I don't expect you to trust me. You see me for the first time and I have an accent. But look — do you really think if you get targeted leads you cannot find anywhere else, at this volume, you will not be successful?"

Yuri Saiko

Key Takeaways
  • Know the current contracts-signed number cold. Use the weekly one on confirmations ("400+ last week").
  • It's a hook to earn attention, not the final argument.
  • Name the accent / newness before they do — then redirect to the proof.
OpeningTrust

The Sale Is Lost 25 Minutes Before the Objection Shows Up

You handled the "I need to think about it" perfectly — and still lost. Because the real miss was at minute two.

Objections at the end are usually symptoms of trust you failed to build at the start. When a prospect asks to "talk to other users" or "think about it," it rarely means they lack information — it means something you did or didn't do early left them unsure. If you diagnose that as a data problem and send references, you're answering the wrong question.

"When people tell me they want to talk to other users, it usually means something I shared — or didn't share — left them unsure. The trust problem started long before the objection."

Yuri Saiko

This is why the opening matters more than the close. Build the human connection, establish credibility, and surface concerns early — so there's no unspoken doubt waiting to detonate as a stall at the end.

Key Takeaways
  • End-of-call stalls are trust debt from the opening coming due.
  • "Talk to other users" = a trust problem, not an information request. (90% never actually call the references.)
  • Invest the first minutes in connection + credibility to prevent the late objection entirely.
OpeningGene's Law

Open With One Real Human Detail

You launch straight into the deck. The prospect braces for a pitch and goes cold.

Open with the genuine detail you found in prep — and mean it. The goal isn't flattery; it's to be a human talking to a human before you're a salesperson talking to a lead. It takes the "corporate" edge off the whole call and buys you the goodwill you'll spend later during the close.

"Find one real detail about the prospect — motorcycles, their niche, a side business, anything genuine. Not fake flattery. Real curiosity. It makes the call less corporate."

Gene Blinkov

Real Demo — Opening on a detail

Rep: Before we dive in — I saw you've been flipping in the Tarrant County area for a couple years now. How'd you get into that side of it?

Prospect: Oh man, long story — started with my uncle's rental…

Rep: [lets them talk — now the call is a conversation, not a demo]

Key Takeaways
  • Real curiosity > scripted flattery. They can tell the difference instantly.
  • Let them talk. The detail is a door, not a checkbox.
  • Goodwill built here is spent later at the ask.
OpeningControl

Set the Structure Up Front — Take Control Early

No frame means the prospect drives, the demo sprawls, and you never reach the ask.

Tell them how the next few minutes will go, and get a micro-agreement to it. A short agenda does three things at once: it signals you've done this before (credibility), it earns permission to ask questions (so discovery doesn't feel like an interrogation), and it lets you legitimately steer back on track when they wander.

Frame the call Here's what I'll do so I don't waste your time: I'll ask a couple quick questions about your business, show you exactly how this would work for your market, and if it's a fit we'll talk about getting you started today. If it's not, I'll tell you straight. Sound fair?

"Sound fair?" is a small no-oriented yes — easy to agree to, and it pre-authorizes both the discovery questions and the close.

Key Takeaways
  • A 15-second agenda earns permission to run the call your way.
  • Include the close in the frame ("if it's a fit, we'll get you started today") so the ask isn't a surprise.
  • Offer the honest out ("if it's not, I'll tell you straight") — it drops resistance.
PART 03

Discovery

Discovery is where the deal is actually built. Not "what do you do" — that's noise. The job is to surface the real trigger, quantify the gap, and get to a budget without putting the answer in their mouth. Every question here is load-bearing.

FrameworkDiscovery

The Most / Least Framework — Find the Real Driver Fast

You ask a dozen surface questions and still don't know what actually moves this person.

Two questions cut through everything: what they want most, and what they're most afraid of. "Most" surfaces the outcome that will make them buy; "least" surfaces the fear you have to neutralize before they will. Once you have both, you know exactly what to amplify and what to disarm — the rest of discovery is confirmation.

The two questions What's the one thing that would make this a home run for you this year? … And what's the thing you'd least want to happen — the part that's made you hesitate on stuff like this before?
Key Takeaways
  • "Most" = the outcome to amplify. "Least" = the fear to neutralize.
  • You can't sell certainty until you know which fear is blocking it.
  • Everything after these two questions is confirmation, not fishing.
DiscoveryListening

Kill Generic Acknowledgments — Prove You Actually Heard Them

"Oh nice, that's great!" tells the prospect you're waiting to talk, not listening.

Replace empty reactions with a specific reflection of what they just said. Generic acknowledgments ("gotcha," "awesome," "makes sense") signal a script running on autopilot. A specific one proves you're present and quietly builds the trust that prevents late-call stalls. This is the same instinct behind Voss's labeling — name what you heard.

Real Demo — generic vs. specific

Prospect: I tried cold calling for six months and burned out — never closed a thing.

Weak: Gotcha, yeah, a lot of people say that. Anyway—

Strong: Six months of dialing with nothing to show for it — that's brutal. So it sounds like the issue was never effort, it was where the leads were coming from.

"When a client gives you an objection or a strange answer, the default reaction should be curiosity, not acceptance or pushback. Always ask why before reacting."

Yuri Saiko

Key Takeaways
  • Reflect the specific thing they said, then label the emotion under it.
  • "Animals react. People think." Pause, paraphrase, re-engage — never fire a rehearsed line.
  • Same objection twice = your strategy is wrong. Change the pattern, don't repeat.
FrameworkVeteran

SPIN — More "What Do You Do" Questions = Worse Close Rate

You spent ten minutes confirming markets and deal types. The prospect is bored and no closer to buying.

Rackham's research on 35,000 calls found Situation questions negatively correlate with closing. "What markets? Wholesale or flip?" adds zero emotional weight. Top performers move fast to Problem and Implication — the questions that make the prospect feel the cost of staying where they are. Run the sequence:

Situation (minimize)
"What markets do you work?" — gather the bare minimum, then move on.
Problem
"You did 25 deals but you're targeting 50 — what's the bottleneck stopping you from just doing more?"
Implication (the killer)
"If you don't find a new lead source by Q2, what happens to that 50-deal target? You're at 8 now — that math gets tight fast."
Need-Payoff
"If a platform gave you 50+ exclusive leads a month in Dallas at $100 each, would that solve the volume problem?"
Real Demo — what Lucas missed (Mar 11 role-play)

Prospect: Did 25 deals last year, want to hit 50. Done 8 so far.

Coach note (Yuri): He literally told you he's behind pace. That's a gift. Lucas stayed on Situation and never unwrapped it — no Implication question, no urgency, deal walked.

Key Takeaways
  • Cut Situation questions to the minimum. They bore experienced buyers and lower close rates.
  • Implication is where urgency is manufactured — connect the gap to a real deadline and cost.
  • When a veteran hands you their target vs. current pace, that math is your close setup.
DiscoveryTrigger

"Why Now? What Shifted?" — Dig Past the Surface Answer

9 out of 10 say "I just saw your ad." That's the surface. It's not the real answer.

The best opener in discovery is "how long have you been thinking about this, and why now?" — followed by one more push past the reflex answer. The real trigger (a divorce, an inherited property, getting burned by the last vendor, scaling pressure) is almost always sitting one question underneath. People give surface answers by default. The pain is there; one more question earns it.

The follow-through question Totally get it — but has something shifted that makes this more of a priority right now versus, say, six months ago?

"'I just saw your ad' is the surface, not the real answer. Push through it — the real trigger is almost always underneath. Five seconds of guts on every call."

TJ Serafini

Key Takeaways
  • "Why now / what shifted" beats every other discovery opener.
  • The first answer is a reflex. The second answer is the truth.
  • Works on B2C demos, B2B partner calls, and re-engagement equally.
DiscoveryLanguage

Cut the Modifier Words — They Leak Doubt

"So this could maybe help you kind of get a few more leads, if that makes sense?"

Softeners like "just," "maybe," "kind of," "a little," "hopefully," and "if that's okay" broadcast that you don't believe your own pitch. They're verbal apologies. Strip them out and the same sentence lands with authority — which is what transfers certainty to the prospect. Filler ("uh," "let me check," "what do I have here") does the same damage and signals low confidence.

Real Demo — same line, doubt removed

Weak: This could maybe get you a few more leads, if that makes sense?

Strong: This gets you exclusive leads in your market that you can't find anywhere else. Here's how.

Every word must earn its place. If you can cut it without losing meaning — cut it. Texts, emails, and scripts equally.
Key Takeaways
  • Kill "just / maybe / kind of / hopefully / if that's okay."
  • Eliminate filler — "uh," "let me check" — it reads as low confidence.
  • Make statements. Certainty can't ride on softened language.
DiscoverySilence

The 2-Second Rule — Let the Silence Do the Work

You ask a good question, then panic and answer it yourself two beats later.

After you ask something that matters, stop talking for a full two seconds — or longer. The instinct to fill silence is where reps talk themselves out of the truth. The prospect will fill it, and what they say in that gap is usually the real answer. This is doubly true after the budget ask and after the close.

"The silence after a question is not your enemy. Sit in it. They will fill it. Every time. Don't put the answer in their mouth."

Gene Blinkov

Key Takeaways
  • Ask, then go quiet. Count to two before you consider speaking.
  • Whoever speaks first after the ask usually loses the point.
  • Most critical after the budget question and after the close.
DiscoveryGene's Law

The Budget Question — Anchor High, Then "Can We Push That Up?"

They say "I don't really have a number in mind" and you blurt "how about $500?" Deal capped.

Never put the number in their mouth, and never anchor low. Ask what their business deserves, sit in the silence, and whatever they say — push it up once. Gene's whole point: if you're comfortable asking for $5,000, you'll find out who can actually pay it. If you anchor at $500, you'll never know.

Instead of "how about $500?" What do you think your business deserves to invest in it to really take off? … [silence] … Okay — can we push that up a little? What do you think about giving it a real shot?

"What? You haven't thought about it? It's your business, buddy. You can't just say 'how about $500?' What if the guy is sitting on $20,000?"

Gene Blinkov

"Nobody asks that question — 'can we push that up a little?' It's uncomfortable. For me it's comfortable. It got Chris Rude so much money. You have no idea."

Gene Blinkov

Timing matters: ask budget after one or two pain questions, not cold. And give a range so they self-select up — "we have deals from $499 all the way to $10,000; assuming you like what you see, what are you comfortable with?"

Key Takeaways
  • Anchor at the top ($5,000). You can always go down — never back up.
  • After any number they give: "Can we push that up a little?" Every time.
  • Ask after 1–2 pain questions, framed as a range. Never cold, never low.
  • "$499 is a good budget to start" is banned — it's the minimum entry, not a good target.
FrameworkDiscovery

Read the DISC Profile — Match Your Pitch to Their Wiring

You run the same pitch on a data-driven operator and a vision-driven newbie. One of them tunes out.

Tag the prospect D / I / S / C in the first few minutes and adjust how you sell — not what you sell. Profile them from prior notes and how they answer discovery, then keep the approach below in a second tab during the demo.

D — Direct / data-driven
High operators, 3–5+ deals/mo. Lead with numbers and ROI: cost per lead, cost per acquisition, target volume. No fluff.
I — Relationship / vision
Often newer. Sell the vision, time savings, community stories. Draw out the pain — lost time, missed deals. Avoid heavy analytics.
S — Risk-averse
Don't want to be sold. Be an advisor. Use case studies. Takeaway close: "might not be for you — let's find out."
C — Numbers / budget
Often a partner dynamic. Frame the cost of inaction. Lead with lead-to-close ratio and timelines. Minimize risk framing.
Key Takeaways
  • DISC changes your delivery, not the offer. Tag them early.
  • D and C want math; I and S want story and safety.
  • Pair it with the profile-matched testimonial for that type.
PART 04

The Pitch

The demo is not a product tour. Its real job is trust and time — enough of both that they'll say yes. Get to the package faster, sell the program not the leads, and tailor the story to who's actually on the call.

The PitchPhilosophy

The Demo's Real Job Is Trust, Not Explanation

You spend 30 minutes walking every filter and menu. They're tired, and no closer to buying.

People forget almost everything you show them. The demo exists to buy you time to build trust — not to transfer platform knowledge. The onboarding/CSM call is where mechanics get taught. On the demo, the more minutes you sit with them as a credible human, the higher your close rate climbs. Explaining the platform for its own sake actively works against you.

"The whole idea of them sitting on your demos is not for you to explain the platform. It's for you to have more time to sell them on the thing we think we can sell them."

Yuri Saiko

"We have a bunch of youngsters with accents who never worked in B2B/B2C sales, going against competitors. I need them to sit more time with you so they trust you more."

Yuri Saiko

Key Takeaways
  • Trust closes deals; feature dumps don't. Optimize the demo for time-with-you.
  • Platform mechanics belong on the onboarding call — say so out loud.
  • A one-hour demo full of explaining usually means the original pitch was weak.
The PitchStructure

Go to the Package Faster — Credibility → Budget → Package → Close

You front-load 30 minutes of platform before ever naming a price. They've checked out by the pitch.

The new demo structure Yuri and Gene landed on is short: credibility, then the budget question, then straight to how their money gets used — then close. Show them where their balance goes, how the coupon applies, and stop. Onboarding handles the rest.

"We explained the platform too long. Go from: what's the budget, how much can you invest — and just show them how they'll use that budget. This is where your balance goes. This is how you apply the coupon. That's it."

Yuri Saiko

Compress the middle After you get started, we sit down together, activate your automated Coupon Club, set your parameters, and you start getting leads. That's the platform walkthrough — on the onboarding call. Right now let's make sure this is a fit and get you rolling.
Key Takeaways
  • Four beats: credibility → budget → package → close. Cut everything else.
  • Show only how their budget gets used, not the whole product.
  • "Can we make it happen, or do you want to start smaller?" — always aim up first.
The PitchYuri's Law

Sell the Program, Not the Leads

You pitch "leads from $29" and get haggled to the floor on price-per-lead.

Leads are a commodity to argue about. The program is a transformation. The bigger package isn't just more leads — it's more bonuses, CSM attention, coaching, community, and lead-backs. Frame the whole ecosystem and the price-per-lead debate disappears. This is also just math: bigger package = more chances = better treatment = higher success.

"Nobody has a smaller package with higher success than the guy with the bigger package. We treat them better. They get more bonuses. They go on CSM calls."

Yuri Saiko

"This is not a lead source. We change people's lives."

Yuri Saiko

Real Demo — reframing off price-per-lead

Prospect: So it's like $29 a lead? PropertyLeads is cheaper on some.

Rep: If this were only about lead price I'd tell you to shop around. But you're not buying leads — you're buying exclusive motivated sellers you can't get anywhere else, plus the coaching, the community, the CSM who fixes your parameters when something's off. The $29 lead is the thing that closed $40K for Jonathan Collins. That's the program.

Key Takeaways
  • Reframe from "leads" (commodity) to "program" (transformation).
  • Bigger package = more bonuses + CSM + coaching + lead-backs. Name them.
  • We treat big spenders differently — say it plainly on the demo.
FrameworkThe Pitch

The "Would That Be Helpful?" Value Stack

You list features. They nod politely and feel nothing.

Turn every feature into a benefit the prospect confirms out loud. Stack value instead of cutting price: name a thing, tie it to their goal, then ask "would that be helpful?" Each yes is a micro-commitment, and the stack builds the certainty that makes the price feel small.

The stack You'd get a month to practice with the AI role-play bot trained on 700 real seller calls — so you're not burning live leads while you learn. Would that be helpful? … You also get monthly coaching with RJ Bates where you can ask anything. Is there any way you wouldn't want that?

"What if you could ask RJ Bates once a month all the questions you got? And that's included. Is there any way you wouldn't pull the trigger on that?"

Yuri Saiko

Key Takeaways
  • Feature → benefit tied to their goal → "would that be helpful?"
  • Each confirmed yes is a brick in the certainty wall.
  • Stack value to shrink price; never cut price to win the moment.
The PitchDemo Element

Walk the Lead-Flow Chain — The One Visual That Sells Quality

Client thinks "Sale leads" means leftovers. You never showed them where leads actually come from.

Yuri's most-skipped high-value demo element: draw the full lead journey, visually, on every call. It pre-empts the sale-leads objection and proves quality before anyone doubts it.

  1. Thousands of digital + cold-call leads with recordings come in.
  2. Quality Hub — AI screens and discards 33–35% of bad leads.
  3. Transcripts and parameters checked.
  4. If a fixed-price buyer exists in that market → lead goes to them instantly (highest quality, fastest path).
  5. If not → marketplace for 24 hours as an exclusive lead.
  6. Unclaimed after 24h → Sale status: same screening, same quality, discounted, non-exclusive, still under the 21-day refund policy.
Sale does NOT mean lower quality. It means 24 hours passed and no one bought it exclusively. The only real difference is speed-to-lead.
Key Takeaways
  • Draw the chain by hand / screen-share on every demo. It's the answer to the sale-leads objection.
  • Analogy: "You get a high-quality lead at a discount because exclusive buyers can't keep up with our volume."
  • Note the AB test: "Sale Leads" is being renamed "Non-Exclusive Leads" — brief clients on exclusivity, not discount.
The PitchControl

Be the Expert — Don't Hand Them Data That Creates Objections

You show live lead volume in a thin county. You just handed them a reason to say no.

More transparency is not more trust. More expertise is more trust. You are the information filter — your job is to contextualize data, not dump it. A feature that removes your ability to frame the conversation is a feature that hurts your close rate. Stop hiding behind the platform; own the sale.

"There is no platform, no mailer, no cold-calling operation that gets you exclusive motivated seller leads in San Diego for $100. Not us, not anyone. You need to expand your geography or raise your price. Here's what I'd recommend..."

Yuri Saiko

That's disqualification with authority — educating, not apologizing. It reads as expertise and it either fixes the expectation or cleanly ends a deal that was never going to work.

Key Takeaways
  • Don't show raw volume/pricing floors that manufacture objections.
  • Contextualize every number — you're the filter, not a dashboard.
  • Disqualify with authority when the market truly can't support them.
The PitchBuyerAgent

Tailor the Pitch — Buyer, Agent, Newbie, Veteran Each Hear a Different Story

You run the wholesaler script on an agent. They don't "close deals" — they "get listings." You've lost them.

The script has role-specific sections. Most reps skip them and default to wholesaler language for everyone. Match the world and the words to who's actually on the call.

Buyer
Highest spend, lowest activation. Sell the identity shift: "Save the $10K assignment fee, call the seller yourself, set your terms. Are you ready to be a closer?"
Agent
They get listings, not deals; no splits on our leads; sellers have stated intent. Educate: no commission, no lock-in, exclusive, pause anytime.
Newbie
Sell certainty + the ecosystem (coaching, community, role-play bot). Don't overload with platform. Get them invested and rolling.
Veteran
Teach/tailor/take control (Challenger). Skip Situation questions. "Your existing sources can't scale linearly past deal 30 — here's the fix."

"You have to go watch podcasts of agents talking to agents in agent language. Same with wholesalers — watch Steve Trang. This is part of your job."

Gene Blinkov

Key Takeaways
  • Agents get listings; wholesalers lock contracts; buyers change identity. Use their language.
  • Buyers = biggest under-exploited segment: highest checks, lowest activation. Pitch them harder.
  • Always ask agents team size + brokerage — group deals available.
PART 05

Trial Closing

Trial closes are how you take the temperature without asking for the sale. Run them throughout the demo — two or three minimum — so you catch objections while there's still time to handle them. This is Gene's mandate and Cardone's playbook, fused.

Trial CloseGene's Law

Trial Close Early and Often — Don't Save It for the End

You present start to finish, then ask one closing question at minute 40 and get blindsided.

"What do you think so far?" must happen 2–3 times per call. Gene flagged "no trial closes" as one of the five things killing the team's demos. Each trial close surfaces hidden resistance while you still have runway to fix it — and reads the room so you don't keep pushing slides at a prospect who's already gone flat.

"Seed the close early: 'If this makes sense, we can start today.' And read the room — stop pushing slides if they're flat."

Gene Blinkov

Key Takeaways
  • Minimum 2–3 trial closes per demo — early, middle, and pre-close.
  • A hesitation on a trial close is a gift: handle it now, not at the end.
  • Flat responses mean stop presenting and start diagnosing.
FrameworkTrial Close

The Cardone 10 — A Trial Close for Every Moment

You only know one closing question, so you use it once and hope.

Ten trial closes, each with a job. Drop them throughout — they tell you exactly where you stand without guessing.

01 · The Temperature Check
"How does this look to you so far?"

Drop it 5–10 minutes in. "Good" = on track. Hesitation = stop and address it now, not at the end.

02 · The Feel Check
"Does this feel like something that could work for your business?"

Feelings close deals, not logic. Moves them from evaluating features to imagining ownership.

03 · The Clarity Check
"Is there anything I haven't explained clearly that's holding you back?"

Smokes out hidden objections without sounding defensive. Frames you as their advocate.

04 · The Timeline Anchor
"If this made sense, would you be the type to start today or wait?"

Reveals their decision pattern before you close. "I'd wait" hands you the real objection early.

05 · The Value Confirmation
"Based on what you've seen — do you see the value here?"

Get explicit agreement on value first. Skip this and you'll wonder why they said no.

06 · The Excitement Read
"What's your excitement level, 1 to 10?"

Below 7 — ask what would make it a 10. 7+ — they're close, ask for the sale.

07 · The Urgency Test
"Is there a reason this wouldn't make sense to start this week?"

Flips the burden — they have to justify NOT starting, which is psychologically harder.

08 · The Partner / Boss Check
"Is there anyone else who'd need to be part of this before you moved forward?"

Ask EARLY, never at the close. Discovering the real decision-maker late is a classic blunder.

09 · The Assumptive Forward
"Once we get you set up — what zip codes would you target first?"

You've assumed the sale without asking. If they answer, they're bought in.

10 · The Recap Close
"So we agree the leads are there, the platform works, and you need more deals — what would stop you from starting today?"

Stack the yes's you've collected, then ask them to justify not buying. The final ask is just connecting the dots.

Key Takeaways
  • Match the close to the moment: temperature early, partner-check early, recap at the end.
  • The excitement read (1–10) gives you a number to work with instead of a guess.
  • After any trial close — stop talking. Let the answer land.
PART 06

The Close

Everything so far was setup. The close is where you ask directly, anchor high, create a reason to move today, and then go silent. No dancing, no "let me know" — ask for the money and work the no.

The CloseGene's Law

Ask Directly for the Money — No Dancing

You soften the ask into oblivion: "So, um, this is a little over budget, but can I tell you why?"

Say the words: buy, invest, start today, card. Being uncomfortable asking for money was one of Gene's five flagged problems. The strength behind the ask signals how much you believe in the product. Weak ask, weak belief, weak close.

"Passable: 'Is there any reason you shouldn't start today?' Better: 'Are you ready to invest in this today?' / 'Visa or Mastercard?' After you ask — stop talking. Silence works."

Gene Blinkov

"It should be with strength — is there any way you can make this one happen? Because loaded investors don't lose this way."

Yuri Saiko

Key Takeaways
  • Use direct verbs: buy, invest, start, card. No hedging.
  • Ask with strength — the energy transfers belief.
  • After the ask: silence. Whoever speaks first loses the point.
FrameworkThe Close

Push the Ceiling Before You Come Down

You open at $499 and they say yes — leaving thousands on the table you'll never recover.

Start at the biggest package. Make the case with conviction. Get a hard "no way" before you offer anything smaller. Once you've handled every objection at the top, sliding down is effortless — the work is already done. And closing below their real budget isn't a win.

"The more you squeeze on the big one, the easier it will be on the small one. Until they told you it's impossible, going down is a very stupid thing to do."

Yuri Saiko

"Maybe it was a $10K budget there. Closing $3K on a $10K budget — nothing to be proud about."

Yuri Saiko

Anchor at $5,000. You can always go down. You can never go back up.
Key Takeaways
  • Exhaust objections at the top package before descending.
  • Ask "is there any way you can make this happen?" — twice, with strength.
  • A close well below budget means you didn't really try. Not a win.
The CloseTechnique

Assumptive & Two-Option Closes — Remove the Yes/No

"Do you want to move forward?" gives them a clean no. Don't offer it.

Close on which, not whether. The assumptive close presumes the sale and asks a logistics question. The two-option close replaces yes/no with a choice between two yeses — then you stop talking.

Assumptive Any reason why we wouldn't move forward and get you set up today?
Two-option (then silence) Most people go monthly, but the annual saves you 50%. Which one do you want me to set up?

"Don't ask 'are we moving forward' — ask 'which one do you prefer, or do you want to start with both?'"

Yuri Saiko

Key Takeaways
  • Ask which, not whether. Both answers are a sale.
  • State the option, then go completely silent.
  • "Do you believe the product can help you, or do you believe you can close?" — the emotional decision point.
The CloseDiscipline

Every Sweetener Needs a Close Condition First

You throw in bonus balance to "help," they take it and still say they'll think about it.

Never give away value without a yes attached. Bonus balance is refundable and costs ISTL real money. Before you offer anything, get the conditional commitment. If the answer to "if I make it work, are you in?" isn't yes — you offer nothing.

"If I can make it work, are you signing up right now? If the answer isn't yes, don't offer anything."

Yuri Saiko

Key Takeaways
  • "Yes if…" comes before any sweetener. No exceptions.
  • Offer lead pack first, bonus balance only if still needed.
  • Sweeteners without conditions train prospects to stall for more.
The CloseUrgency

Client-First Urgency — Give a Real Reason to Move Today

"Let me lock it in for you" is transparent. They see the tactic and it backfires.

The reason to act today must serve the client, not you. Make it concrete and ROI-framed. Real scarcity ("spots left," promo ending) works when it's true and specific; discount-framing signals desperation, so frame the cost of waiting instead.

"This promo gets you 20 extra leads compared to the standard package. That's a potential game changer on ROI."

Yuri Saiko

"You should say: my manager just sold today, it's rolling, people are signing up — and actually tell them, 17 spots left."

Yuri Saiko

If price is the wall, financing is the bridge: available from $1,499 via AfterPay, Affirm, Klarna. "Most people find a way when they decide they want to — where do you think you could find it?"

Key Takeaways
  • Urgency must benefit the client (extra leads/ROI), not just "lock it in."
  • Discount-framing = desperation. Frame the cost of waiting.
  • Financing from $1,499 turns "too much" into "which plan."
The CloseDiscipline

Draw the Line — Then Exit Clean to Follow-Up

The deal's not closing today, so you linger, over-explain, and sour the relationship.

Make one strong, direct ask for today. If it's a genuine no, don't grovel — set the follow-up and exit clean. A well-placed "no today" that preserves the relationship is worth more than ten minutes of desperate pushing that burns it.

The clean line Totally fair. Last thing — is there any way to make this work today, given the bonus comes off tomorrow? … No? No problem at all. Here's what I'll do: I'll send you the recording of that Dallas investor, and I'll check in Thursday. Sound good?
Key Takeaways
  • One strong ask for today. If no, transition to a scheduled follow-up.
  • Never end on "let me know" — sell the specific next step.
  • "Not yet" / "I need to think" is where the sale starts — but sometimes it starts on the follow-up.
PART 07

The Objection Library

The reference section you jump to mid-call. First the meta-principles that govern every objection, then the six categories — each with the framework, the exact language, and the real ISTL version Yuri and Gene use. An objection is a buying signal. Treat it like one.

FrameworkFoundation

The Meta-Principles — How to Handle Any Objection

You hear an objection and fire back a rehearsed line. That's reacting, not selling.

Every objection runs the same formula, and most are symptoms of something you missed earlier.

LISTEN → ACKNOWLEDGE → AGREE → ISOLATE → REFRAME → TIE TO VALUE → CLOSE
  • Clarify first, don't react. Answering instantly reads as defensive. Ask what they mean before you handle it.
  • Make statements, don't just ask. You cannot overcome an objection by only asking questions — assert value.
  • Prevention beats reaction. An objection is usually a broken earlier step. Hunt objections early ("see anything that concerned you?") and file them.
  • Isolate. "Besides that, is there anything else holding you back?" Get the full list before handling any single one.
  • Same objection twice = change strategy. They've mentally checked out; break the pattern, don't repeat the line.
  • An objection is a buying signal. "Is that the best you can do?" means they're ready — restate value, don't discount.

"Animals react. People think. Every objection — pause, paraphrase, re-engage. Don't fire back a rehearsed line."

Yuri Saiko

"You cannot deal with an objection if you don't really believe this will be beneficial for the guy."

Yuri Saiko

The One Line
  • "The platform isn't expensive — buying the wrong leads is."
Objection · ALow-Budget

"It's Too Expensive" / "I Don't Have the Money"

The most common objection — and the most misread. Price is rarely the real issue.

Isolate whether they can't afford the product or the solution. Then reframe against the cost of staying stuck. If they say no to a small entry too, the issue is trust, not price — use the descending commitment ladder to find out.

Product vs. solution (Jin's frame) When you say it's too much — is it that the product feels expensive, or that the solution to your lead problem feels expensive? Because not fixing this is costing you way more than the package every month.

"You spent $60,000 to close one deal? Why didn't you change faster?"

Yuri Saiko

The $19 reality check reframes price-to-quality without insulting: "Higher quality leads require higher investment. The $19 lead and the $200 lead aren't the same thing." And the descending ladder: offer smaller; if they decline $100 too, it was never about the money.

Handling Moves
  • Isolate product vs. solution. Reframe against cost of inaction.
  • Descending ladder → a "no" to $100 reveals a trust problem, not a price one.
  • Financing from $1,499. "$499 is the minimum entry" — never "a good start."
  • Sometimes "too expensive" = "not my customer." Not every objection needs overcoming.
Objection · BTiming

"I Need to Think About It" / "I'm Not Ready Yet"

The polite exit. ~90% who say "next week" never move. Handle it now.

Diagnose the fear beneath the stall, then neutralize timing with "buy now, use later." "I need to think" usually means "I don't trust the product" or "I don't trust myself" — label it and find out which.

Voss label + no-oriented question It sounds like you want to be sure you can fully commit before spending money. Would it be a terrible idea to lock in the bonus pricing today and just let it sit on your account until you're ready?
Buy now, use later You can purchase today and it sits on your balance — it won't burn, it's not going anywhere. But the bonus is only available today.

For the "not ready" newbie, don't sell leads — sell the ecosystem (coaching, community, role-play bot). And isolate the timing: "Other than the timing, is there anything else holding you back?"

Handling Moves
  • Handle it now — "next week" is almost never real. Ask "any way to make it work this week?"
  • Label the fear (product vs. self), then buy-now-use-later to remove timing.
  • "Not ready" → pitch ecosystem, not leads. Offer the no-commitment demo/soft agreement.
Objection · CAuthority

"I Need to Talk to My Partner" / "I Want to Talk to Other Users First"

Both are usually trust in disguise — and ~90% who ask for references never call them.

Separate permission from validation. "Talk to my spouse" — are you asking for permission or for support? "Talk to other users" — that's a trust gap that started earlier in the call, not an information request. Diagnose it, don't send a reference list.

"When people say they want to talk to other users, it usually means something I shared — or didn't share — left them unsure. What was the most concerning thing from our conversation?"

Yuri Saiko

Yuri's strategic-withdraw follow-up (Sandler + Klaff status play): call back and lower your status. "I actually thought about our conversation and don't think I was as helpful as I could've been." The guard drops, the real objection surfaces. Ask the partner/boss check EARLY next time — never discover the decision-maker at the close.

Handling Moves
  • Permission vs. support — ask which. "Talk to users" = trust gap, diagnose it.
  • Strategic withdraw: apologize, re-diagnose, offer a 3-way call with your manager.
  • Accusation audit on the callback: "You're probably thinking I'm just calling to sell you again."
Objection · DNeed / Fit

"I Can Do This Myself" / "Just Send Me a Proposal"

Both dodge the decision. Quantify the gap before they leave the call.

"I can do this myself" = smart enough to, but at what cost in time? Reframe: two years learning to generate leads vs. results next month. "Just send me a proposal" = an objection wearing a request's clothes — address it now, not on a next call that won't happen.

DIY reframe You're clearly capable of building this yourself. The real question is time — do you want to spend the next two years learning lead gen, or start closing deals next month while we handle the lead side?
"Send me a proposal" Happy to — but proposals are just words on a page. What specifically would need to be in it for you to say yes? Let's solve that right now while we're both here.
Handling Moves
  • DIY → reframe to time-cost and opportunity cost, not capability.
  • "Send a proposal" → surface and handle the real objection on this call.
  • Gap Selling: if you didn't find the gap, you can't close it. Quantify current vs. desired state.
Objection · ETrust

"I've Heard Bad Reviews" / "I Got Burned by a Lead Company Before"

The objection beneath the objection is fear of being burned again. Answer the emotion before the logic.

Don't deny — contextualize, and diagnose the real fear. Label it (Voss), make them do the math on bad reviews, and use the bucket technique to find whether they fear wasting money or fear the process.

Label first (Belfort: the objection beneath) It sounds like you've been burned before by a platform that overpromised, and you don't want to repeat that. Help me understand — what specifically happened last time?

"20 bad reviews out of 5,000 investors who closed. Do the math — the system works. But tell me what you saw, because I want to address the real concern."

Yuri Saiko

When they blame the last vendor's leads, gently put process back on the table — "the leads usually aren't the issue, conversion is; what did your follow-up look like?" — without over-promising (over-inflated expectations lead to faster refunds). Then land the profile-matched proof: it hits 10x harder because it answers a fear they just named.

Handling Moves
  • Label the fear, ask "what happened last time," THEN present proof.
  • Contextualize bad reviews with the ratio — don't deny them.
  • Don't over-promise; put some responsibility on process. Address, don't inflate.
Objection · FCompetition

"Another Provider Is Cheaper" / "I Already Use PropertyLeads"

Don't trash the competitor. Lean in, find what they dislike, and own the moat.

Ask what they've considered, met, liked, and disliked — then never pitch a feature they liked elsewhere that you can't match. Lead quality is similar everywhere; the moat is transparency + the ability to adjust.

"I don't care what they like about the competitor. I want to know what they don't like."

Yuri Saiko

The adjustability moat Lead quality is roughly the same everywhere. The difference is you can filter by motivation, square footage, ownership length — and if it's not working, we adjust it with you. With them, you spend and hope. If it doesn't work, then what?

Position list tools honestly: DealMachine / PropStream / PropWire sell prospect lists, not motivated leads. "Lists = you pay with time. Motivated leads = you pay with money." Stay evenhanded — trust comes from being fair, not from bashing.

Handling Moves
  • Lean into competitor mentions: considered / met / liked / disliked.
  • Adjustability + transparency is the moat. "You spend and hope" is the contrast.
  • Never bash. Evenhandedness builds the trust that closes.
Objection · GNewbie

"Do I Have to Pay Upfront?"

Lucas lost a prospect by confirming payment. The answer is always no — keep them in the ecosystem.

Never confirm they have to pay upfront. Never flinch. There's always a free path in: spin the wheel daily, join webinars, get free bonus balance. Redirect to showing them how.

"You don't have to pay anything. Want me to show you how to do it? Spin the wheel, join the webinar, get free balance."

Yuri Saiko

Then land a proof point of a free-path win — Zach's spin-the-wheel free balance turning into an $11K closed deal. Nobody leaves that call when you frame it this way.

Handling Moves
  • Answer is always "no, let me show you how." Keep them in the ecosystem.
  • Free paths: wheel, webinars, bonus balance.
  • Close with a free-path success story (Zach → $11K).
PART 08

Follow-Up & No-Shows

Up to 90% of revenue lives in the follow-up. The average deal closes after 6–8 touches — yet most reps quit after one. This is the unglamorous engine room where consistent money is actually made.

Follow-UpDiscipline

Follow-Up Is Where the Money Is — Treat It Like a Product

You send one "just checking in" text and give up when they don't reply.

Up to 90% of revenue comes from follow-ups, and the average close takes 6–8 touches. That means the deal you "lost" on the demo is usually still alive. Block a fixed hour every day for follow-ups — same time, non-negotiable — and never end a call with a vague "let me know." Sell the next step like it's part of the product.

"It's boom — it's one hour on follow-ups. Not maybe follow-up. Everything else works around the block, not the other way around."

Yuri Saiko

The standard the top reps actually hit is 10–20 tracked touches per deal. If you're not tracking follow-ups in Zoho, you're flying blind — pull calls + tasks per deal and know your number.

Key Takeaways
  • 6–8 touches to close on average; 10–20 is the top-rep standard.
  • Fixed daily follow-up block — same hour, protected.
  • Always send proposals: 5 texts + 5 emails. Email rarely closes; the phone does.
FrameworkFollow-Up

The Follow-Up Script Structure — Re-Deliver Value, Don't Chase

"Hey, the promo expires today!" — they don't remember the value, so the urgency means nothing.

Every follow-up rebuilds value before it asks for anything. Run the sequence in order:

  1. Break the ice — "How did the call go? Was everything clear?" Let them talk first.
  2. Drop credibility — "Did they mention we signed 400+ contracts last week?" A hook, not a closer.
  3. Re-anchor the package — get them to say yes to the value again before money.
  4. Surface objections — find the real blocker before piling on incentives.
  5. Only then, extras — bonus balance, coupons, urgency come last.

"If you call and just say the promo is expiring, they don't remember the value. You lost before you started."

Yuri Saiko

Key Takeaways
  • Value first, incentive last. Never open with "the promo's ending."
  • Every touch needs a reason to exist — a clip, an interview, an update.
  • Conversations over CTAs: create a reason to reply.
FrameworkExisting Clients

The 3-Step Existing-Client Sequence

You cold-call an existing client with "how's it going" and it turns into a complaint session.

Plant value before you ever ask for anything. Never open with "how are the leads going" (you'll get a complaint) or "you're low on balance" (screams sales call).

Step 1 · Send something useful
A specific training clip or interview matched to where they are. "FYI — we recorded a guy who closed $150K in three months in your market. Here's the link — let me know what you think."
Step 2 · Follow up on it
"Did you get a chance to watch that?" A warm, natural reason to call. The seed is planted.
Step 3 · Now make your move
Once they're in a positive frame: "We have a promotion for existing clients this week — same package, but I can add X. Want me to set it up?"

"If someone calls and says 'I just want to check in on you' — that's a bank call. Nobody books a call with their bank to chat. Every call needs a specific reason."

Yuri Saiko

Key Takeaways
  • Content → follow-up → offer. Never lead with the ask.
  • Say "recording / interview / training," never "testimonial."
  • Low-balance call: start positive ("send any offers yet?"), never "why didn't you call them."
FrameworkNo-Shows

The 5-Touch No-Show Sequence

A demo no-shows and you mark it dead. It was warmer than any cold lead you'll dial today.

No-shows already raised their hand once. Work them on a schedule, not a whim.

  1. T + 1 hour — reschedule attempt while intent is warm.
  2. T + 1 day — value hook (send a relevant clip, not "you missed our call").
  3. T + 3 days — county-specific social proof.
  4. T + 7 days — soft offer.
  5. T + 14 days — nurture / long-term.

Prevent them upstream with a confirmation sequence: text 24h before, call 2h before, and get a hard verbal yes at the moment of booking. Keep booking windows to 5–7 days — long windows breed no-shows. The calendar-invite title matters too: "$50 bonus" showed 59% vs. 39% for "strategy session."

Key Takeaways
  • Chase no-shows personally, on a 5-touch schedule — never generically.
  • Confirm 24h + 2h + hard verbal yes at booking. Short windows.
  • Monday no-shows come from Thu/Fri bookings — cover weekends.
Follow-UpRelationship

Get Them to Save Your Number — and Keep Every Text Short

Clients don't answer because they don't know who's calling. On webinars, most can't name their account manager.

If a client hasn't saved your number, the relationship doesn't really exist. Make it part of onboarding: "Write down my number — I'm your account manager. I'll send you exclusive coupons, free coaching invites, and early access through this number. You don't want to miss those."

"Tell your GPT explicitly: no fluff, no sophisticated words, short sentences. One sentence on what's in the video and why it's relevant beats three sentences of filler."

Yuri Saiko

Build a GPT chat per client: save their context and generate the personalized pre-call text (Anna's system — flagged by Yuri as best on the team). And on confirmation calls, always drop the contracts-signed number.

Key Takeaways
  • Make saving your number a standard onboarding step, with a concrete reason.
  • GPT texts: short, plain, one specific reason to engage. If it sounds like an ad, rewrite it.
  • Watch the material yourself so you can reference "the part at the 12-minute mark."
PART 09

Retention, CSM & Winback

Churn is now a comp metric — retention is money in your pocket, not a nice-to-have. Existing clients are the fastest revenue on the board, and lapsed subscribers are the highest-ROI list you'll ever dial.

RetentionCSM

Existing Clients Are the Fastest Money on the Board

You chase cold leads all day while your own book of buyers sits untouched.

They already gave you money and the relationship exists. When a promo runs, they should be your first call of the day. The CSM call has three goals in order: save the account, upsell, book the next demo. Don't try to take more money from someone who hasn't closed yet — give them something extra first, then close.

"Every call to an existing client needs a specific value hook. Not a check-in. A coupon, a webinar, a new product, a free session. Lead with something concrete."

Yuri Saiko

Key Takeaways
  • CSM order: save → upsell → book demo.
  • Promo day = existing clients first; they can buy immediately.
  • Push FPM / Coupon Club right after close — a package alone isn't "active."
RetentionComp

Retention Is a Comp Metric — Own Your Book

You close a client and move on. Then they churn and it hits your commission.

A churn multiplier means retaining clients directly affects what you earn. The last rep to sell a client owns retention for ~90 days. Around 40% of churn is voluntary and addressable — 60% of cancellations are card declines, not intent to leave, so fight for those. Schedule mandatory FPM check-ins from activation: it stops the constant pinging, catches bad parameter setups before they become refunds, and creates a natural upsell moment.

"Bumping from $150 to $200 would get you 3–4 leads a week instead of 1–2."

Yuri Saiko

Key Takeaways
  • You own retention for 90 days after the sale. Retaining = earning.
  • Card-decline churn is winnable — chase it, don't write it off.
  • Mandatory FPM check-ins prevent refunds and open upsells.
RetentionWinback

Winback — The Highest-ROI List You'll Ever Dial

You ignore cancelled and empty-balance clients. They're the easiest yeses left.

Lapsed subscribers already bought, already believed, and just drifted. The ego cost of re-entry is near zero. Ron's dead-simple play closed two on the spot: "Looks like your balance is empty — want more leads?" No pitch, no objection, just timing. Reframe ISTL as a marketing channel: good marketers keep spending and adjust; they don't quit after one imperfect month.

"Looks like your balance is empty, want more leads?"

Ron Horchak

Key Takeaways
  • Winback is the highest-ROI activity in any subscription business — make it a daily list.
  • Empty-balance Coupon Club member = a package pitch (they have buying power).
  • Reframe as a marketing channel to reset "it didn't work" thinking.
